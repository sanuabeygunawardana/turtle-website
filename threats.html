Threats to turtles

Six of the seven species of sea turtles are considered threatened or endangered around the globe as a result of human behavior and way of life. There are many ways that people harm these creatures, from people catching them with fishing gear to people consuming their flesh and eggs.

Sea turtles are tough creatures. They have swum the waters since dinosaurs walked the earth, protected by special body armor from their natural foes. But in recent years, rapidly expanding human populations have led to fresh, intense pressures, making it harder than ever for sea turtles to survive.

5 major threats to turtles

Fisheries bycatch

Thousands to tens of thousands of sea turtles are said to perish each year as a result of the fishing business. Turtles caught in gill nets, trawls, and longlines are discarded as bycatch. Additionally, the disturbance to their food source and environment has an effect on those that are able to evade fishing nets.

Coastal development

The habitats of sea turtles are being lost every year as a result of receding coasts. Every time a new hotel or high-rise is constructed close to the shore, whenever the sea bottom is dug up, and whenever the beach erodes, sea turtle food sources and nesting grounds suffer greatly.

Pollution and pathogens

Sea turtles are frequently harmed by marine pollution. Sea turtles are injured by ingestion and entanglement due to plastic pollution, fishing gear left behind, petroleum byproducts, and other debris. In addition to disrupting nesting behavior and hatchling orientation, ocean pollution can impair the turtles' immune systems.

Direct take

Turtles are murdered all over the world and sold as unusual food, oil, leather, and jewelry on the international market. Millions of hawkbill turtles alone have been slaughtered over the past century only for the value of their shells. Even while efforts to promote conservation have resulted in a decline in the worldwide trade in luxury and handicrafts, turtles continue to face danger in some regions of Africa , Asia, and the Americas.

Climate change

The extent to which sea turtles can be impacted by climate change is only now coming to light. Climate change has the potential to alter the natural sex ratios of hatchlings, raise the risk of disease outbreaks, and increase the frequency of extreme weather conditions that devastate coral reefs and beaches used for nesting.
